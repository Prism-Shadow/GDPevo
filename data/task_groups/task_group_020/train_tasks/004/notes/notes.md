The standard answer identifies six priority issues. Employee transfer is high severity because the draft requires offers to only 70% of employees while seller retains WARN and severance exposure; the required position is buyer comparable offers to all business employees and buyer assumption of the relevant employment burden. TSA/service continuity is high severity because the draft provides only a 90-day TSA for accounting and payroll, while the deal needs a 12-month transition across ERP, payroll, finance, regulatory, IT, procurement, and quality records; the latest client fallback is 9 months only with a dedicated buyer migration team. Restrictive covenants are high severity because the draft imposes a five-year Atlas-and-affiliates restriction over all industrial hand tools; the required limit is three years maximum, limited to the divested line and existing geographies, with any non-solicit no broader than divested-business customers and employees. IP transition is high severity because the draft has an 18-month trademark phase-out plus broad retained design-file access; the expected position is a maximum 12-month trademark phase-out and a narrow transitional license with retained-IP boundaries. Escrow is medium severity because the 12.5% general escrow exceeds the 10% seller carve-out target. Closing deadline is high severity because the October 31, 2026 outside date is 42 days after September 19, 2026 signing, below the 60-day escalation threshold and below the 75-day client preference while consents and TSA schedules remain open.

The output schema uses `priority_issues` and `transition_flags`. The evaluator has six exact-match scoring points with raw weights totaling 14: employee transfer (3), TSA/service continuity (3), restrictive-covenant limits (2), IP transition period and scope (2), escrow plus closing deadline quantification (2), and the priority issue severity/action set (2). Numeric comparisons are deterministic at the declared precision. `required_service_codes` is compared as a set. `priority_issues` is evaluated by `issue_id`, `severity`, and `recommended_action`, independent of incidental evidence ordering. The evaluator accepts a prediction path as its first argument and defaults to this task's `output/answer.json`.

Likely model pitfalls include trusting the generic template provisions, treating the 90-day TSA as acceptable because accounting and payroll are covered, omitting ERP or IT service continuity, missing the WARN/severance retention problem, accepting a five-year affiliate-wide covenant as normal M&A language, treating broad retained design-file access as harmless trademark use, using 60 days instead of the client's 75-day target for the final output flag, or overlooking that the 12.5% escrow is a seller-side economic deviation even though it is less central than the transition issues.

Transfer design: as a train task, this is a formal calibration task, not a tutorial. By attempting it and comparing against the answer, a solver can infer reusable task-group habits: prioritize active documents over stale template records, compare counterparty draft clauses against the applicable client playbook and latest client instructions, convert legal deviations into controlled severity/action fields, quantify dates and percentages exactly, and distinguish transition-operational risks from ordinary economic terms.
